Enrolling a Renewable Energy Project under Argentina's RIGI Regime

Report Overview

A practical guide for foreign developers on enrolling renewable energy projects under Argentina's Régimen de Incentivo para Grandes Inversiones (RIGI), which offers 30-year tax, customs, and foreign-exchange stability for investments of at least US$200 million.

Key Eligibility Requirements

  • Minimum Investment: US$200 million, with at least US$80 million disbursed within 24 months after approval
  • Sector Coverage: Energy (solar, wind, hydro, and storage)
  • Application Window: Two years from the Base Law's entry into force (August 2024 - July 2026) with possible 12-month extension
  • Vehicle: Single-Project Vehicle (VPU) or local branch owning all project assets

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Project & Corporate Set-Up: Incorporate VPU/branch in Argentina and register with tax (AFIP) & corporate registries
  2. Permitting Alignment: File Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) at provincial level
  3. Investment Plan Preparation: Draft RIGI investment plan detailing capex, technology, schedule, financing, and local-content (≥20% from Argentine suppliers)
  4. Formal Application: Upload via Ministry of Economy (MoE) portal under Res. 1,074/2024
  5. Technical & Legal Review: MoE verifies eligibility, capex, and funding sources
  6. Approval & Certificate Issuance: MoE issues "Certificado de Adhesión al RIGI" granting 30-year stability
  7. Post-Approval Compliance: Invest ≥US$80 million within 24 months and file semi-annual progress reports

Core Documentation Checklist

  • Corporate pack (notarized bylaws, board minutes, tax ID)
  • Technical dossier (resource study, equipment list, interconnection studies)
  • Financial plan (capex breakdown, sources & uses, proof of funds)
  • EIA/DIA (filed copy or approval resolution)
  • Affidavits (minimum-investment commitment, AML/KYC compliance)
  • Customs annex (list of goods to be imported duty-free)
  • FX annex (projected export proceeds and foreign-currency needs)
Timeline: Review period typically ranges 3-6 months. The 30-year stability starts on certificate date, not on commercial operation date. Example: YPF's "El Quemado" 305 MW solar project took approximately 3 months from application to approval.

Argentina – Post-Rejection Options for RIGI Applications and Implications for Other Incentive Regimes

Report Overview

Analysis of remedies available when a RIGI application is rejected or returned for corrections, and implications for accessing Argentina's alternative incentive frameworks.

Immediate Remedies for RIGI Filing Rejection

  • Administrative "Subsanación" Window: Comité Evaluador typically requests additional data or adjustments before definitive refusal (example: McEwen Copper's Los Azules revised and resubmitted after regulator feedback)
  • Resubmission Within Legal Window: Projects can file again any time before the window closes (initial 2-year period to July 2026, extendable by one year)
  • Formal Administrative Appeal: Right to challenge first-instance decisions through standard hierarchical appeals (15-30 business-day deadlines)
  • International Arbitration Fallback: Once admitted, RIGI grants access to international arbitration for disputes

Strategic Considerations for Resubmission

  • Timing: Lost time may compress ability to invest US$80 million within two years of approval
  • Parallel Work: Continue engineering, EIA, and financing work to enable rapid execution once approval secured
  • Structure Review: Review SPV bylaws against decree 749/2024 before refiling if shareholding, fiscal domicile or capitalization issues caused setback
  • Provincial Coordination: Engage provincial mining/energy ministries early; align EIA timeline with RIGI resubmission

Impact on Alternative Incentive Regimes

  • Renewable Energy Promotion (Law 26,190/27,191): Projects rejected under RIGI remain eligible provided they haven't renounced other benefits
  • Provincial Regimes: Failed national RIGI bid doesn't impede access to provincial tax stability agreements
  • Sector-Specific Schemes: May combine with provincial tools if benefits don't overlap
  • FX and Export Duty Concessions: Other regimes rarely match RIGI's progressive free FX availability (20%/40%/100%) and three-year export duty holiday
Risk Management Best Practices: Conduct early gap analysis; maintain dual-track incentives review; ensure documentation discipline with audited capex estimates; maintain stakeholder communication about iterative feedback cycles.

Regulatory Framework

Impact of Argentina's Recent Regulatory Changes on Foreign Investment in Renewable Energy

Report Overview

Analysis of how Argentina's post-2024 regulatory changes affect foreign investors developing renewable energy projects, focusing on improved bankability, fiscal benefits, and remaining challenges.

Key Regulatory Changes Since 2024

  • RIGI (Law 27,742 & Decree 749): 25% corporate tax (vs. 35%), 30-year stability on tax/customs/FX, 100% retention of export proceeds from year 3, free FX access for debt service and dividends (min. capex ≥ US$200M)
  • Energy Secretariat Resolution 400/25: Liberalizes Wholesale Electricity Market (MEM), introduces Term Market (MAT), denominates spot revenues in USD, allows bilateral B2B PPAs
  • Resolutions 150/2024 & 21/2025: Repeal excessive state/CAMMESA intervention, set transition rules toward cost-reflective pricing
  • Act No. 21505: Raises national renewable generation target to 70% of total output by 2030 and extends tax incentives

Opportunities for Foreign Developers

  • Enhanced Revenue Certainty: 20-year CAMMESA PPAs remain available, while Resolution 400/25 unlocks additional USD-based spot and bilateral sales
  • Lower Fiscal & FX Friction: RIGI reduces tax rates, abolishes most export duties, freezes fiscal regime for 30 years
  • Improved Bankability: FODER guarantees, RIGI FX protections, and dollarized revenues collectively de-risk project debt
  • Demand Tailwinds: 70% renewable generation target and 20% consumption quota ensure strong procurement pipelines

Challenges and Risks

  • Minimum Ticket Size: RIGI benefits only apply to projects ≥ US$200 million
  • Transition Uncertainty: Legacy peso-linked PPAs will migrate to liberalized MEM; pricing and dispatch rules during hand-off remain under consultation
  • Macroeconomic Volatility: Construction costs and local O&M expenses remain peso-denominated
  • Permitting Enforcement: New milestone rules require developers to show progress or risk license revocation

Strategic Considerations for Market Entry

  • Hybrid Revenue Stacks: Combine RenovAr PPA with MAT sales or corporate PPAs
  • RIGI VPU Structure: Ring-fence assets in Single-Project Vehicle to maximize tax and FX benefits
  • Local-Currency Funding: Pair international project finance with local bank facilities for peso cost components
  • Advance Procurement: Leverage accelerated depreciation and import-tariff relief for early equipment ordering
Overall Impact: Argentina's post-2024 regulatory changes meaningfully improve the commercial landscape for foreign renewable investors by pairing strong demand growth with fiscal, currency, and market reforms, positioning Argentina as a more bankable destination for utility-scale wind, solar, and storage.
